Neural thicket ensembles (\randopt) offer a computationally efficient way to create task-specific experts from pretrained models by sampling random weight perturbations. While these ensembles improve accuracy on clean data, their vulnerability to adversarial and out-of-distribution (\ood) inputs remains an open question. In this paper, we investigate the robustness of neural thicket ensembles on reasoning tasks. We find that standard Gaussian perturbations can actually degrade performance on \ood inputs, as the sampled experts often become over-correlated and share similar failure modes. To address this, we propose \methodname, a simple yet effective sampling strategy that pairs opposing weight perturbations to explicitly encourage diversity. We evaluate our approach on a custom \rgsm benchmark encompassing clean, adversarial, and \ood distribution shifts. Our results demonstrate that \methodname significantly enhances \ood robustness, improving accuracy to 15.0\% compared to 5.0\% for standard ensembles, while simultaneously matching or exceeding their performance on adversarial and clean data. These findings highlight that weight-space diversity is critical for safe ensemble deployment, and that antithetic sampling provides a memory-efficient path to resilient neural thickets.
